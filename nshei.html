Reflection · Shabbos Nachamu

Comfort Comes Slowly

The music is back. The Nine Days are behind us. This Shabbos is called Shabbos Nachamu — the Shabbos of comfort — but comfort, like everything worthwhile, tends to arrive gradually rather than all at once.

Comfort Comes Slowly

Shabbos Nachamu takes its name from this week's Haftorah: "Nachamu, nachamu ami — comfort, comfort My people." Chazal noticed the word is doubled, and asked why.

The Nine Days have ended. Tishah B'Av has passed. Music is permitted again, haircuts are back on the calendar, and the heaviness of the last few weeks starts to lift. But it doesn't lift all at once — and that's exactly the point.

Chazal teach that the double language of "comfort, comfort" hints at two kinds of nechama: comfort for what was lost in the past, and comfort in knowing what's still ahead. Real comfort isn't a switch that flips. It's a process, the same way grief is a process and growth is a process.

🌸 A Season of Seven

The seven weeks between Tishah B'Av and Rosh Hashanah are known as the "Sheva D'Nechemta" — the seven weeks of consolation, each with its own Haftorah of comfort and hope. It's not a coincidence that this stretch leads straight into the Yomim Noraim. The comfort we build now is what carries us into the new year.

☀️ Permission to Exhale

For those who kept the halachos of the Nine Days carefully — no music, no meat, no laundry, no haircuts — this Shabbos can feel like a deep exhale. Put on the Shabbos playlist again. Let the kids run around to a niggun. Notice how good it feels, and let that feeling itself be part of the avodah.

💛 Carrying the Lesson Forward

The goal was never to just get through the Nine Days and forget them. The longing we practiced — for the Beis Hamikdash, for a world that is whole — doesn't have to disappear the moment the fast ends. We can carry a little of that awareness into ordinary weeks: a bit more gratitude for what we have, a bit more sensitivity to what's still missing in our own lives and communities.

Nachamu, nachamu ami. May this be a summer of real comfort, and may we merit to see the ultimate nechama speedily in our days. 🕊️

A weekly series · Hilchos Shabbos

Halacha Corner — The 39 Melachos

Starting this week, we'll be going through the 39 melachos of Shabbos, one at a time, to build a working familiarity with the categories of melacha and why they matter. We begin with the first: Zoreah — planting.

🌱
Melacha #1 of 39

Zoreah — Planting

Zoreah is the melacha of causing something to grow — placing a seed, root, or cutting into soil (or a similar growing medium) so that it will take root and develop. It's the first of the eleven melachos connected to preparing bread, all of which are traced back to the labor that went into building the Mishkan.

In practical terms, this is why watering a plant, replanting a cutting, or even placing cut flowers in a way that encourages new roots to form can raise real Shabbos concerns — even though none of it looks like "farm work" in the way we usually picture it.

This is a general overview for awareness and learning only — not a halachic ruling. Practical questions (watering plants, handling flowers, gardening near Shabbos) should always be brought to your rav.

Divrei Torah

🕊️
Parshas Va'eschanan · Shabbos Nachamu

Two Words, Repeated on Purpose

Why does the Haftorah open with "Nachamu, nachamu" instead of just "Nachamu"? Because comfort that comes only from Above isn't enough — we're meant to comfort each other too.

💬
A closer look

Comfort is a two-way street

Many meforshim explain the doubled "nachamu" as Hashem comforting Klal Yisrael, and Klal Yisrael in turn being asked to comfort one another. A phone call to a friend, a kind word to someone still carrying something heavy — these are small acts of nechama that echo the theme of the week.

🌿
A closer look

Why now, and not right after the fast?

Shabbos Nachamu falls the Shabbos immediately after Tishah B'Av — not weeks later. The comfort doesn't wait for the sadness to fully fade; it begins the moment we're ready to start rebuilding, even while the ache is still fresh.

A new occasional feature spotlighting notable women in Jewish history — their lives, their courage, and what we can learn from them today.

👑
This week's spotlight

Nechama Leibowitz

A woman whose name means "comfort" — fitting for Shabbos Nachamu — Nechama Leibowitz (1905–1997) became one of the most influential Torah teachers of the 20th century, known for her handwritten "gilyonos" (study sheets) mailed to students around the world, corrected personally and sent back with detailed feedback, often at her own expense. She taught Tanach to generations of students in Yerushalayim with rigor, warmth, and an insistence that every question be backed by a close reading of the text itself. Her method still shapes how Tanach is taught today.

Small tricks, big difference

Kitchen & Home Tips

Little hacks passed down from Bubbies, neighbors, and years of trial & error. Send us yours — we'll add them to the collection.

🕯️
Shabbos prep

Cool soup faster before Shabbos

Pour hot chicken soup into a wide 9x13 pan instead of a tall pot. Cools in 30 minutes instead of 3 hours — and it's safer to move to the fridge before Shabbos.

🧂
Bakery hack

Rescue oversalted soup

Peel a raw potato, cut in half, and drop into the pot. Simmer 15 minutes, then remove. The potato absorbs excess salt.

🥶
Freezer wisdom

Freeze challah dough, not baked

Shape and freeze raw challah on a tray. Transfer to bag once frozen. Take out Thursday morning to thaw and rise — you get bakery-fresh challah every week.

🍅
Meal prep

The Sunday "protein prep"

Every Sunday, bake 3 chickens, hard-boil a dozen eggs, and make a huge pot of rice. You have easy lunches and quick suppers all week without cooking dinner from scratch.

✨
Erev Shabbos

The 15-minute kitchen reset

Set a timer for 15 minutes right after licht bentchen prep. Wipe counters, load dishwasher, sweep floor. It doesn't have to be perfect — just done. Shabbos feels 10x calmer.

🍋
Bakery hack

Extend your fresh herbs

Wrap fresh parsley or dill in a slightly damp paper towel, then put inside a plastic bag in the fridge. Stays fresh 2 weeks instead of 3 days.

🧽
Home & hearth

Baking soda + vinegar for the sink

Weekly: pour ½ cup baking soda down the drain, then 1 cup white vinegar. Wait 15 min, flush with hot water. Prevents clogs, kills smell.

👶
Mother's helper

The "5-minute pickup rule"

Every night before bed: everyone in the house picks up for 5 minutes only. Kids too. In a week you save hours of deep-cleaning — and teach kids the habit.

🎒
Back to school

Start the countdown now

Make a simple checklist by child: uniform/clothing sizes, shoes, backpack, and supplies. Shop early — the best sizes and colors go fast once August hits. Set a per-child budget ahead of time so trips stay quick and stress-free.

📅
Back to school

Reset the schedule a week early

Start shifting bedtime and wake-up time about a week before school starts, so the first day isn't also the first early morning. A trial "first day" routine run-through helps younger kids especially.

For the little ones

Kids' Corner — No Electronics Needed

Ideas to save your sanity on long summer afternoons and hot Shabbos weekends. Grouped by situation — pick a tab, pick an idea, and go.

All ages

Parsha Charades

Pick a scene from the parsha. Act it out silently. Family guesses. Best for Yaakov and the sheep, Yosef and the dreams, the makos.

Ages 5+

Story chain

One person says a sentence to start a story. Next person adds one sentence. Keep going around the table — the sillier it gets, the better.

Ages 4+

Alef-bais scavenger hunt

Go through the house pointing to objects in Hebrew alphabet order. Kids compete to be first. Bonus: make a written list.

All ages

Guess who

One person thinks of a person from Chumash or a family member. Others ask yes/no questions to guess who. First to guess wins.

Ages 6+

Card castles

Deck of cards + hours of quiet focus. Whoever builds the tallest before it falls wins. Great for competitive kids.

Ages 3+

Family show & tell

Each kid picks a favorite item and does a mini "presentation" — where it came from, why they love it. Toddlers eat it up.

All ages

Backyard ice hunt

Freeze small toys in ice cube trays. Kids "excavate" them with spoons and warm water. Cools them down and buys you 45 minutes.

Ages 3+

Sidewalk chalk mural

Give each kid a section of the sidewalk. When done, walk the whole family through the "art gallery" and give awards.

Ages 5+

Lemonade stand

Set up out front. Real money, real customers. Kids learn making change, customer service, and marketing. Great mitzvah opportunity: donate to tzedakah.

All ages

The nature bingo card

Make a bingo card of things to find outside: red leaf, feather, three types of rocks, a bug, a puddle. Walk the block. First to bingo wins.

Ages 4+

Backyard obstacle course

Chairs, cushions, hula hoops, a broomstick. Have older kids design it. Time each other. Bonus: video the runs.

All ages

Sprinkler paintbrush

Give kids paintbrushes and buckets of water. Let them "paint" the fence, the driveway, the siding. Water evaporates — endless canvas.

Ages 4+

Sock puppet theater

Old socks + markers + buttons + yarn. Kids make puppets, then perform a show for the family after supper. Curtains: a blanket over two chairs.

Ages 3+

Restaurant at home

Kids write "menus," set the table, take orders. Serve real snacks. Amazing pretend-play. Great vocabulary building.

All ages

Fort under the dining table

Sheets, chairs, cushions, flashlights. Read books inside. Serve lunch in the fort as a special treat. Instant magic.

Ages 5+

Cardboard box marble maze

Cut open a cereal box or shoe box. Kids glue in cardboard pieces and straws to make a marble maze. Design + engineering + hours of play.

Ages 3+

Sensory bin

Big plastic tub + rice or dry beans + small toys hidden inside + scoops and cups. Kids can play for an hour. Contain to one room!

Ages 6+

Kid Iron Chef

Give kids 3 safe ingredients from the pantry. They "compete" to make a snack. You judge. Winner gets to pick supper for that night.

Ages 1-3

Muffin tin sort

Give a muffin tin and a bowl of pom-poms, cheerios, or small blocks. Toddler "sorts" by color into the tin. 30 quiet minutes.

Ages 2-4

Water pouring station

Two cups, one bowl of water, tray underneath. Toddler pours water back and forth. Improves fine motor. Bring towels.

Ages 1-3

Sticker page

Sheet of stickers + blank paper. Toddlers can peel and stick for a shockingly long time. Buy stickers in bulk on Amazon.

Ages 2+

Play-dough tray

Homemade play-dough (flour, salt, water, food color) + cookie cutters + a rolling pin. Set them up at the table. Save the tray for next time.

Ages 1-2

Basket of "safe" kitchen tools

Wooden spoons, plastic measuring cups, silicone spatulas, small pot. Give them their own drawer in the kitchen. Endless fascination.

Ages 2-4

Painter's tape road map

Painter's tape on the floor becomes roads. Toy cars zoom around. Easy to peel up after. Endless configurations.

All ages

Sprinkler tag

Run the sprinkler in the backyard and play regular tag — anyone who runs through gets a cool-down burst. Simple and endlessly fun on a hot day.

Ages 4+

Water balloon toss

Pair up, fill a bucket of water balloons, and toss back and forth, taking a step back after each catch. Last pair with an unbroken balloon wins.

All ages

Backyard car wash

Buckets of soapy water, sponges, and a hose. Let the kids "wash" bikes, scooters, or the actual car. They'll stay busy (and cool) for an hour.

Ages 3+

Kiddie pool sensory play

Fill a small kiddie pool with water, cups, and floating toys. Add a few drops of food coloring for "potion making." Easy to set up on the porch or driveway.

Ages 5+

Water relay race

Teams race to fill a bucket using only cups carried overhead from a starting point. First full bucket wins. Great for a group of cousins or friends.

All ages

Ice block excavation

Freeze small toys in a large bowl of water overnight. Give kids warm water, spoons, and salt to "melt" their way to the treasure. Perfect for a shaded porch.

A thought to carry with us

This Week's Chinuch Nugget

This week

Practicing Comfort Out Loud

This week, try modeling comfort for your children the way Shabbos Nachamu models it for us — out loud, and on purpose. Ask each child: "Who do you know that might need a little extra kindness this week?" Then help them do one small thing about it — a card, a call, a compliment. Comfort isn't just something we receive; it's something we're meant to give away.
